# /three-percent

> "I modify objects only 3%, because that's where the interestingness lies — in slight modification, not replacement." — Virgil Abloh

The 3% rule is Virgil's most powerful creative tool. You don't rebuild. You don't start over. You find the smallest change that shifts perception completely. A zip tie. A quotation mark. A word on the back. An arrow pointing to nothing. 3%.

This skill takes what you have and finds the exact intervention.

## When to run this

- You have something that's not landing and you don't know why
- You're about to throw everything out and start over (don't)
- You need to differentiate without a rebrand
- You have a product, copy, design, or idea that feels "almost"
- You're stuck in perfectionism and not shipping

## Workflow

### Step 1 — What do you have?

Ask the user to describe what exists. Can be:
- A product or product name
- A piece of copy or headline
- A design or layout
- A brand or campaign
- A pitch or proposal
- A piece of content
- A business model

Get the current state. Be specific. "A sneaker" is not enough. "An all-white low-top with a gum sole, no branding, targeting 18-25 streetwear buyers" is enough.

### Step 2 — The Inventory

Before finding the 3%, catalog the 100%:

List every element of the thing as it exists:
- What it looks like
- What it says
- What it's made of
- What it's called
- How it's distributed
- Who it's for (stated)
- What category it lives in

Don't evaluate. Just inventory. See it completely before you touch it.

### Step 3 — Find the 3%

Now find the single intervention. The rules:

**It must be small.** If your change takes more than a day to execute, it's not 3%.

**It must shift meaning, not just appearance.** A color change is decoration. A word change can be philosophy.

**It must create a double-take.** The viewer should look once, process it normally, then look again and see something different.

**It cannot require explanation.** If you have to explain why the 3% is interesting, it's not the right 3%.

Run through these lenses to find candidates:

**LANGUAGE:** Is there a word you could add, remove, or put in quotes that changes everything? What if the product name had quotation marks? What if you put the wrong word on the right thing?

**MATERIAL:** Is there one material swap — just one — that changes the entire perceived category? Luxury material on a utilitarian object. Utilitarian material on a luxury object.

**SCALE:** What if you made it 3% bigger? 3% smaller? What if just one element was wildly out of scale?

**CATEGORY:** What if you named it something from a completely different category? Called a rug "furniture." Called a sweatshirt a "sculpture." Called a price tag "art."

**ABSENCE:** What if you removed one thing everyone expects to be there? The logo. The price. The explanation. The instruction.

**ADDITION:** What if you added one thing that has no business being there? A zip tie. An arrow. An asterisk. A footnote.

### Step 4 — The Proposal

Give exactly three 3% options. For each:

```
THE INTERVENTION: [Specific change]
WHAT IT SHIFTS: [How perception changes]
HOW TO EXECUTE: [What you actually do, in one day or less]
TOURIST READS IT AS: [What a newcomer sees]
PURIST READS IT AS: [What an expert sees]
```

### Step 5 — Make the call

Pick one. Don't hedge. Say which one and why.

End with: **What are you keeping? What are you changing?**

## The Principle

Virgil said he started doing this "because he was getting tired." But what he discovered was that the 3% is where cultural meaning lives. The gap between what something is and what it almost is — that gap is where interestingness lives.

You're not making a new thing. You're making the existing thing undeniable.
