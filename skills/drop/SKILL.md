# /drop

> "I'm not interested in selling. I'm interested in the moment people see it." — Virgil Abloh

A drop is not a launch. A launch is a press release. A drop is an event — a moment in culture where something appears, demands attention, and rewards the people who showed up.

Off-White, Supreme, Yeezy, A-COLD-WALL, even Apple at its peak — they didn't release products, they staged drops. The release model for sneakers, the model for albums, the model for limited everything. This skill turns whatever you're shipping into a drop.

## When to run this

- You're about to launch something and the plan is "tweet about it"
- You have a product, feature, essay, video, app, or release with no momentum strategy
- You want to create demand, not just satisfy it
- You're shipping into a noisy market and need to cut through
- You want the launch to mean something culturally, not just commercially

## The Principle

A drop has four properties that a launch doesn't:

1. **Urgency** — There's a window. After the window closes, you missed it (or paid 5x for it).
2. **Scarcity** — Real or perceived limitation. Not everyone gets one.
3. **Story** — A reason it exists now, this way, with this person. The story is the product.
4. **Ritual** — A repeated form people learn to anticipate. The drop has a shape.

Without all four, it's a launch. Launches are forgotten on Monday.

## Workflow

### Step 1 — What are you dropping?

Get specific:
- What is it? (one sentence)
- Why does it exist? (the story — not the press release version)
- Who's it for? (not "everyone")
- When does it appear? (date and time, not "soon")
- How long is the window? (hours? days? one week? forever-but-this-version-disappears?)

If any of these are vague, fix them before going further. A drop with vague answers is a launch in a costume.

### Step 2 — Engineer the four properties

**URGENCY**
- Pick a moment. Not a week. A specific day, ideally a specific hour.
- What disappears if they miss it? (price tier? edition? bundle? bonus? access?)
- What is the countdown clock pointing at?

**SCARCITY**
- What's limited? (units, time, access, geography, configuration)
- Is the scarcity real? If not, don't fake it. Audiences smell artificial scarcity.
- Real scarcity options: numbered editions, time-limited variant, regional release, founder-only access, signed/personalized, original-source-material run.

**STORY**
- One sentence: why this, why now, why you?
- The story is not "we built a thing." The story is the lineage, the obsession, the constraint, the collaborator, the moment.
- Best stories: a creator you've never collabed with, a constraint you imposed, a reference no one expects, a flaw made on purpose, a personal stake.

**RITUAL**
- What's the shape that repeats? (Thursday drops at 11am ET. Quarterly issues. Every full moon. After every product ships.)
- Even if this is your first one, design the ritual. The second drop is easier when there's a form.
- Ritual builds anticipation between drops. The audience learns to wait.

### Step 3 — The Tease

Drops require a pre-game. The tease is part of the drop.

Choose 1–3 of these (don't do all of them — restraint is part of the signal):
- **The teaser image** — One frame. No copy. No date. Just the thing, partially obscured.
- **The cryptic post** — A line. A symbol. A number. Audiences love decoding.
- **The whisper list** — Email/DM list of people who hear first. Membership signals belonging.
- **The collaborator reveal** — Drop the name of who you made it with before you drop the thing.
- **The countdown** — A timer. A landing page that shows nothing but the time remaining.
- **The leak** — Intentionally let one detail slip in a way that travels.

The tease should make people text each other. If no one's texting about it, it's not teasing — it's noise.

### Step 4 — The Drop Moment

The actual release. Design it like a stage entrance:

- **The page** — Where does it live the moment it's live? URL, layout, what loads first.
- **The signal** — How do followers know it just dropped? (post the moment it's live, not 10 min later)
- **The form** — What action does the audience take? (buy / DM / subscribe / share / book)
- **The proof** — How do early people prove they were there? (screenshot, photo, edition number, "drop receipt")

### Step 5 — The Aftermath

The drop doesn't end when the inventory sells out. The aftermath is part of the drop:

- **Document who got one** — Repost buyers/users wearing/using it. The community is the product now.
- **Show what sold out** — Scarcity is a story you tell after, not just before.
- **Tease the next one** — The current drop's aftermath is the next drop's pre-game.
- **Don't restock** — Or if you do, restock with a variation that makes the original the original.

### Step 6 — The Drop Plan

Deliver:

```
THE THING: [What's dropping, one sentence]
THE STORY: [Why this, why now, why you]
THE DATE: [Specific moment, with timezone]
THE WINDOW: [How long it's available]
THE SCARCITY: [What's limited and how]
THE TEASE: [1–3 pre-drop moves with timing]
THE DROP MOMENT: [Page, signal, form, proof]
THE AFTERMATH: [Documentation + tease for next]
THE RITUAL: [The shape this drop creates for future drops]
```

## The Deeper Principle

A launch begs for attention. A drop demands it. The difference isn't budget or audience — it's belief. You believe this thing deserves a moment, so you build a moment for it.

If you can't bring yourself to give your work a moment, the work itself isn't ready.

The question isn't: how do we get people to notice?

The question is: what would make people stop what they're doing and pay attention right now?
