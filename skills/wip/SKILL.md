# /wip

> "Sample sale was the museum. Work in progress is the brand." — Virgil Abloh

> "Failure doesn't exist. Failure is as real as Halloween ghosts." — Virgil Abloh

Off-White's signature was the construction visible. Zip ties left on. "FOR WALKING" printed on the boots. Quotation marks around the word "SHOELACES." The design hid nothing. The design was the process.

WIP is Virgil's antidote to perfectionism. The cure for "not ready yet." The permission slip to ship the rough version, learn from contact with reality, and iterate in public — because hiding the work until it's "done" is the slowest way to make anything good.

This skill takes the thing you haven't shipped because it's not ready, and ships it.

## When to run this

- You've been "almost done" with this for more than two weeks
- You're polishing instead of shipping
- You're scared to put it out because someone might see the seams
- You have a beta, draft, prototype, sketch, or v0.1 sitting in a folder
- You believe you need to wait until it's perfect (you don't)
- You're optimizing in private when you could be learning in public

## The Principle

Three core moves:

1. **Show the construction.** The rough edges aren't a bug, they're a signal. Visible process is honesty. Honesty is rare. Rare is interesting.

2. **Ship to learn, not to launch.** The version you put out today is data collection for the version you put out next month. You cannot get this data from your own brain. You have to ship to get it.

3. **No failure exists.** Every shipped thing teaches you something. The only failure is not shipping. Refusing to ship is the actual failure dressed up as caution.

## Workflow

### Step 1 — Name the thing you haven't shipped

Ask the user: what do you have that's not out yet?

Get specific:
- What is it? (product, essay, video, app, feature, talk, page, prototype)
- How long has it been "almost ready"? (be honest)
- What's the stated reason for not shipping?
- What's the real reason?

The stated reason is usually a quality concern. The real reason is usually fear. Both are valid signals. Both are also why you should ship today.

### Step 2 — Define done for v0.1, not v1

You're not shipping the finished thing. You're shipping the version that lets the world tell you what to make next.

For your specific WIP, write down:

**WHAT MUST WORK** — The single thing that has to function for this to be testable.
**WHAT CAN BE BROKEN** — Everything else. Yes, really. List the broken stuff and ship anyway.
**WHAT THE AUDIENCE WILL FORGIVE** — In WIP mode, audiences forgive a lot — IF you're honest about it. They will not forgive pretending the broken parts are intentional.

Done for v0.1 is: the audience can experience the core idea and react to it.

That's it. That's the bar.

### Step 3 — Make the construction visible

Don't hide the rough edges. Frame them.

Pick the WIP signals that fit your medium:

**FOR PRODUCTS / APPS:**
- Label it "v0.1" or "beta" — not as an apology, as a flex
- Show the changelog publicly
- Have a "what's broken" page (yes, really)
- Ask for feedback explicitly, with a specific question

**FOR WRITING / CONTENT:**
- Title or subtitle the rough version honestly ("Rough cut," "Thinking out loud," "Draft")
- Mark unfinished sections rather than deleting them
- Publish thinking, not conclusions
- Show the references in the margin

**FOR DESIGN / BRAND / VISUAL:**
- Leave the construction visible (annotations, grid, scaffold, guidelines)
- Show the alternates you didn't pick
- Caption with the constraint or process
- Date-stamp the version

**FOR TALKS / PITCHES:**
- Open with what you're still figuring out
- Show one slide that's clearly a placeholder
- Ask the audience the question you don't have an answer to yet
- Promise an updated version after you learn from this one

The signals say: this is real, it's moving, you're seeing it before it's polished, and that's the point.

### Step 4 — Ship it today (or this week, hard limit)

Set the moment. Don't push it. The whole exercise dies if "ship by Friday" becomes "ship next Friday."

Pick:
- **Where** does it go? (URL, post, repo, stage, meeting)
- **Who** sees it first? (don't aim for "everyone" — aim for the smallest audience that can give you useful signal)
- **What's the WIP signal** you're attaching? (label, caption, subtitle, header)
- **What feedback** are you specifically asking for? (one question, not "thoughts?")

Then ship it. While you read this. Open the tab. Hit publish.

### Step 5 — Iterate in public

The WIP move only works if the iteration is also visible. Otherwise you're just shipping a bad first version.

Schedule:
- **The check-in** — Within 1 week of shipping, post what you learned and what's changing.
- **The next version** — Within 2-4 weeks, ship v0.2 with visible improvements informed by the feedback.
- **The repeating ritual** — Make iteration a public habit. The audience starts to anticipate the updates and the work grows in public.

Iteration in public has a compounding effect: each version recruits more attention than the last because people are watching the trajectory.

### Step 6 — The WIP Plan

Deliver:

```
THE THING: [What you're shipping]
WHY IT'S NOT "DONE": [The honest reason]
WHAT MUST WORK FOR v0.1: [The single must-have]
WHAT CAN BE BROKEN: [Everything else, listed]
THE WIP SIGNAL: [How you frame it as intentionally unfinished]
SHIP MOMENT: [Specific day, this week]
SMALLEST USEFUL AUDIENCE: [Who sees it first]
THE QUESTION YOU'RE ASKING THEM: [One specific question]
THE NEXT VERSION DATE: [When v0.2 ships]
```

## The Deeper Principle

The instinct to wait until something is perfect is actually a refusal to be in a relationship with your audience. You're choosing the version of the work that lives in your head over the version that lives in the world.

Virgil understood that the audience completes the work. Not by participating in it, but by reacting to it. You can't get to the finished thing in private — the finished thing requires public friction to become itself.

Work in progress isn't an excuse for low quality. It's a refusal to wait for permission.

The question isn't: is it ready?

The question is: what version of this can I put out today that lets me learn what to do next?

Now ship it.
