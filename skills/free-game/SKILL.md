# /free-game

> "Free game. Unlocking doors without expecting anything in return." — Virgil Abloh

> "I want a kid in Accra or a kid in Mexico City or a kid in Columbus, Ohio to be able to see themselves in what I'm doing." — Virgil Abloh

Virgil called it "Free Game." The idea: share everything you know, not as a marketing play, but as a genuine belief that information should travel. He posted his process. He answered DMs. He taught at Harvard. He put his design thesis online. He gave it all away — and it made him more valuable, not less.

The paradox: the more you give away, the more indispensable you become. Because what you know isn't your competitive advantage. How you think is.

## When to run this

- You've learned something that would have saved you months if you'd known it earlier
- You have a process, framework, or way of thinking that only lives in your head
- You want to build an audience, a community, or a reputation without advertising
- You're doing interesting work that no one knows about because you haven't said anything
- You want to create something that outlasts the immediate business context

## The Principle

Virgil's Free Game came from a specific belief: gatekeeping is insecurity. When you hoard knowledge, you're operating from scarcity. When you share it, you're operating from abundance — and you signal that you have more where that came from.

The Free Game formula:
1. Extract the insight (not the surface behavior, the underlying principle)
2. Translate it (make it legible to the 17-year-old AND the PhD)
3. Put it in a form that travels (tweet, lecture, method, tool, course, skill)
4. Release it without a conversion goal

The last point is critical. Free Game with a sales funnel attached isn't Free Game. It's a lead magnet.

## Workflow

### Step 1 — What do you actually know?

Ask: what have I figured out that most people in my field haven't?

Not "what am I good at." What have I specifically learned — from failure, from experimentation, from doing it wrong first — that would change how someone approaches this?

List:
- Counterintuitive lessons (things that turned out to be the opposite of what you expected)
- Process discoveries (the order or method that makes something work)
- Permission slips (things you can do that people don't realize they're allowed to do)
- Hard-won shortcuts (things that save time, but only if you know they exist)
- Mental models (frameworks that let you see clearly in situations that usually create confusion)

### Step 2 — Find the transferable principle

Surface behavior isn't Free Game. The underlying principle is.

Bad Free Game: "I use Figma for everything."
Good Free Game: "The tool doesn't matter. Commitment to the constraint does. Pick one tool and exhaust it before switching."

Bad Free Game: "I worked 20-hour days when I was starting out."
Good Free Game: "The 3% rule — modify existing things slightly rather than building from scratch. Iteration is faster than origination and the result is often better."

For each item you listed, ask: what's the principle underneath this?

### Step 3 — Pick the format

Free Game travels in specific forms. Choose the right container:

**THE LESSON** — One thing you learned, stated directly. "Here's what I got wrong for two years before I figured this out."

**THE METHOD** — A repeatable process with steps. Not theory — the actual sequence.

**THE FRAMEWORK** — A way of seeing a category or problem. Give it a name. The name makes it portable.

**THE COMPARISON** — Two approaches. Here's what most people do vs. here's what actually works.

**THE CASE STUDY** — One project, start to finish. Show the work. Show the wrong turns. Show the outcome.

**THE TOOL** — An artifact that embeds the knowledge. A template, a checklist, a script, a skill. The knowledge is in the object.

### Step 4 — Write the Free Game

Structure:

```
THE INSIGHT: [One sentence — what you actually know that most people don't]
WHERE IT CAME FROM: [The specific experience that taught you this]
THE WRONG WAY: [What people usually do / what you did before you knew this]
THE RIGHT WAY: [What you do now / the actual method]
THE UNDERLYING PRINCIPLE: [The abstraction — why this works]
WHO NEEDS THIS MOST: [The specific person this changes everything for]
THE FORMAT: [How this should be packaged to travel]
```

### Step 5 — Release it

Free Game only works if you actually give it away. Not gated behind an email. Not "book a call to learn more."

Decide:
- Where does this live? (social post, long-form essay, YouTube video, GitHub repo, skill file, course, lecture, DM)
- Who sees it first? (the community you're part of, not the audience you want)
- How do you know it landed? (someone uses it, shares it, or tells you it changed something)

Then release it. The goal is not attribution. The goal is that the idea travels.

## The Deeper Principle

Virgil said he was operating for the 17-year-old version of himself. The kid who didn't have access. Who needed someone to show them that the door was already open.

Free Game is a belief system before it's a strategy. You give it away because the alternative — hoarding it, selling it, protecting it — is a lesser version of yourself.

The question isn't: what's valuable enough to share?

The question is: what do I know that would change everything for the right person, and why haven't I said it yet?
