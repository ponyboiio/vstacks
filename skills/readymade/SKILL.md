# /readymade

> "Duchamp is my lawyer. He gave me the art-historical grounds for absorbing pre-existing intellectual property." — Virgil Abloh

> "A pair of socks can be made into high fashion using intellect." — Virgil Abloh

Marcel Duchamp put a urinal in a museum in 1917 and called it "Fountain." He didn't make anything. He recontextualized everything. Virgil Abloh understood this as the foundation of modern creativity — the readymade is not laziness, it's the highest form of conceptual thinking.

You don't need to create from scratch. You need to see what already exists and know what it could become.

## When to run this

- You're building something new but feel like "it's all been done"
- You have raw material — a product, a template, a process, a category — that feels generic
- You need a concept and you're staring at a blank page
- You have something existing that isn't resonating and you don't know why
- You want to enter a category without being swallowed by it

## The Principle

Virgil linked streetwear to Duchamp: "This idea of the readymade — you take something that's overlooked and put it in a museum context and now it's art."

The readymade requires three things:
1. **Selection** — You have to see it. Most people walk past readymades every day.
2. **Recontextualization** — Place it somewhere it doesn't belong. That displacement creates meaning.
3. **Authorship** — The act of choosing IS the creative act. Curation is creation.

## Workflow

### Step 1 — Take inventory of what exists

What's already in the space? List:
- What products/ideas/formats already exist in this category
- What's been dismissed as too simple, too obvious, too cheap, too utilitarian
- What does everyone in this space use but no one talks about
- What's the "boring" infrastructure that everyone takes for granted

### Step 2 — Find the overlooked object

The readymade is always hiding in plain sight. Look for:

**The Utilitarian:** What functional object is being used without being seen? (The zip tie. The shipping label. The receipt. The price tag.)

**The Vernacular:** What language, symbol, or form is so common it's invisible? (Helvetica. The checkbox. The asterisk. "New.")

**The Institutional:** What format has authority but no beauty? (The invoice. The terms of service. The warning label. The instruction manual.)

**The Cultural:** What reference is so ubiquitous it's stopped being noticed? (The flag. The uniform. The trophy. The handshake.)

### Step 3 — The Displacement

Now move the object. Put it somewhere it doesn't belong.

Ask:
- What happens if this appears in a luxury context?
- What happens if this appears in a fine art context?
- What happens if this appears at massive scale?
- What happens if this appears with a price tag that contradicts its perceived value?
- What happens if you put quotation marks on it?

The displacement creates the question. The question creates the engagement.

### Step 4 — The Authorship Statement

Every readymade needs an authorship statement — the frame that tells the audience how to see it.

This can be:
- A title (Duchamp's "Fountain")
- A word printed on the object ("SCULPTURE" on a handbag)
- A context (placing it in a gallery, a flagship store, a museum)
- A price (the $499 IKEA rug with "Keep Off" — the price makes it art)
- A story (the Free Game platform — the medium is the message)

What's the authorship statement for this readymade?

### Step 5 — The Proposal

Deliver:

```
THE FOUND OBJECT: [What already exists that we're working with]
THE DISPLACEMENT: [Where we're putting it / what context we're changing]
THE AUTHORSHIP STATEMENT: [The frame that makes it intentional]
TOURIST READS: [How a newcomer experiences it]
PURIST READS: [What the expert sees underneath]
THE EXECUTION: [How to actually make this happen]
```

## The Deeper Principle

Virgil said: "Streetwear is linked to Duchamp." What he meant was: the culture that remixes, samples, and recontextualizes is doing exactly what the art world's most radical thinker did. The DJ, the streetwear designer, the meme creator — they're all readymade artists.

The question is never "where do I find something original?"

The question is: "what am I walking past every day that deserves to be in a museum?"
