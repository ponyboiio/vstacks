# /tourist

> "The tourist is curious, ambitious, public — wanting to scale big ideas. The purist is steeped in knowledge, gatekeeping, expert. I operate in the gray area between them." — Virgil Abloh

Experts miss what newcomers see. Insiders miss what outsiders see. The tourist walks into any space — fashion, architecture, music, business — without the baggage of knowing how it's supposed to be done. That's a superpower.

This skill applies the tourist lens to your work. It's not naivety. It's strategic outsider thinking.

## When to run this

- Your work is built for an audience that doesn't look like you
- You've been working on something so long you can't see it anymore
- You need to know if a "civilian" would understand this immediately
- You're about to present something and want to pre-empt confusion
- You're entering a space you don't come from (new industry, new audience, new market)

## Workflow

### Step 1 — Establish the insider frame

What is the creator's context?
- What do they know?
- What do they take for granted?
- What language do they use without thinking?
- What would they never think to explain because it's obvious to them?

Build the insider's mental model of the work.

### Step 2 — Enter as the tourist

Now completely drop the insider frame. You are:
- Arriving in this space for the first time
- Genuinely curious, not dismissive
- Carrying your own references from elsewhere
- Not knowing the rules — so not following them

The tourist is not stupid. The tourist is Virgil walking into fashion from architecture. The tourist is someone who has deep expertise — just in a different field.

Ask: **What field is the tourist coming from?** (If not specified, use: a 17-year-old with no industry knowledge and an honest reaction.)

### Step 3 — The Tourist Audit

Walk through the work as the tourist and report:

**FIRST IMPRESSION (3 seconds)**
What hits first? What's the immediate read before any thought? Don't filter this.

**WHAT I DON'T UNDERSTAND**
List every assumption the work makes that a tourist wouldn't share. Every piece of jargon. Every reference. Every "of course."

**WHAT I FIND INTERESTING THAT YOU DON'T**
What has the creator stopped seeing because they've looked at it too long? Often the thing that makes a tourist stop is the thing the insider glosses over because it's "just how it's done."

**WHAT DOESN'T MAKE SENSE**
Not "what's wrong" — what's confusing. There's a difference. Wrong can be fixed technically. Confusing needs a translation.

**THE QUESTION I WANT TO ASK**
The one question a tourist would ask that an insider would never think to answer because it seems too obvious.

**THE THING I'D TELL MY FRIEND**
How the tourist would describe this to someone who knows nothing about it. This is your real value proposition — not the one you wrote, but the one a stranger extracts.

### Step 4 — The Translation Layer

Now build the bridge. For each gap the tourist found:

```
INSIDER ASSUMPTION: [What the creator takes for granted]
TOURIST CONFUSION: [What's actually unclear]
THE TRANSLATION: [How to close the gap without dumbing it down]
```

The goal is never to make it simpler. The goal is to make it speak to two audiences at once — the tourist who's curious AND the purist who knows everything.

### Step 5 — The Tourist Verdict

One of three outcomes:

**LANDS IMMEDIATELY** — The tourist gets it in 3 seconds. No changes needed. Ship it.

**LANDS WITH ONE EDIT** — One translation needed. Name it specifically.

**DOESN'T LAND** — The work is inside-out. Built for the creator, not the audience. Start with the tourist's first question and rebuild from there.

## The Principle

Virgil entered fashion from architecture. He entered luxury from streetwear. He entered fine art from graphic design. Every time, he was the tourist. And every time, that outsider status let him see what the insiders couldn't — not because he didn't know the field, but because he wasn't captured by it.

Being the tourist is a choice. Make it deliberately.
