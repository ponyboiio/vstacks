# /figures

> "I work in the gray area between tourist and purist. Both are valid. Both must be served." — Virgil Abloh

> "Figures of speech." — title of Virgil's MCA Chicago retrospective

The hardest thing in any creative discipline is making work that lands twice — once for the newcomer who has no context, and again for the expert who knows everything. Most work picks a side: dumbed down for mass appeal, or insider-coded for the cognoscenti. Both are losses.

Virgil called his retrospective "Figures of Speech" because all of his work was a figure — a thing that means one thing literally and another thing in context. A pair of socks called "SOCKS." A logo with quotation marks. A reference no one needs to get, that doubles the thing if you do.

This skill takes work that's working on one level and adds the second level.

## When to run this

- Your work lands with experts but flies past everyone else
- Your work hits the mass audience but the people whose opinion you respect think it's basic
- You're trying to enter a sophisticated category without losing accessibility
- You have a thing that's almost there but feels too obvious OR too obscure
- You're building for two audiences at once and feel like you have to choose

## The Principle

Two-level work is built on a literal layer that anyone can read, and a figurative layer that rewards the people who know.

The literal layer cannot be confusing. The figurative layer cannot be required.

Get this wrong in one direction: the work is sophisticated and inaccessible. Wrong in the other: the work is broad and forgettable. Get it right: the work is undeniable on first contact AND deepens on inspection.

The reference, the joke, the wink, the lineage — these are not necessary to enjoy the work. They are gifts to the people who notice.

## Workflow

### Step 1 — Locate the work on the spectrum

Show me what you're working on. Then ask: where does it currently sit?

```
[TOURIST ONLY] ---------- [BALANCED] ---------- [PURIST ONLY]
```

Mark its current position honestly:

- **Tourist only** — Anyone gets it. Nobody who knows the field finds it interesting. (Most marketing, most consumer brands, most "designed for everyone" anything.)
- **Purist only** — Experts love it. Newcomers bounce off. (Most academic work, most insider art, most niche tools.)
- **Balanced** — Lands at first contact, deepens on closer look. (Off-White, Apple at its peak, the best bands, the best films, the rare brand that crosses over.)

### Step 2 — Diagnose what's missing

**If tourist-only:** What sophistication can be added without breaking accessibility?
- An unexpected reference (in the credits, the colophon, the back of the tag, the easter egg)
- A material or process choice that experts will recognize but doesn't change the surface
- A name or word that rewards research
- A collaborator the tourist won't know but the purist will respect
- A constraint visible only to people who've tried this before

**If purist-only:** What surface clarity can be added without dumbing it down?
- A literal label that names the thing plainly
- An entry point that a 17-year-old can grab in 3 seconds
- A visual hook that doesn't require knowledge
- A second-channel version (social cut, short-form, plain English summary)
- An invitation, not a wall — make the depth optional, not mandatory

### Step 3 — Build the two layers

Now design the work as a pair of reads:

**THE LITERAL LAYER (what the tourist sees)**
- What does someone with no context see in 3 seconds?
- What does it look like, say, do — at face value?
- This must work on its own. If it doesn't work without the figurative layer, you're hiding behind references.

**THE FIGURATIVE LAYER (what the purist sees)**
- What's the reference, the lineage, the joke, the choice that the literal reader misses?
- What's the wink to the people who know?
- This must reward, not gate. The depth makes the work better for those who notice — it doesn't punish those who don't.

### Step 4 — The Tourist Test and the Purist Test

Run the work through both audiences. Be specific about who they are:

**TOURIST TEST**
- Pick a specific person who doesn't know this field. (Roommate. Parent. 17-year-old cousin.)
- Show them the work for 5 seconds.
- Ask: "What's this?" Don't lead the witness.
- Pass: they have an immediate read, even if shallow.
- Fail: confusion, hesitation, or "I don't get it."

**PURIST TEST**
- Pick a specific expert in the relevant field. (The person whose taste you trust most.)
- Show them the work.
- Ask: "What do you notice?"
- Pass: they catch the second layer and respect the choice.
- Fail: they think it's basic or derivative.

If either test fails, you don't have a figure yet. You have a one-level thing.

### Step 5 — The Figure

Deliver:

```
THE WORK: [What you're making]
THE LITERAL READ: [What the tourist sees in 3 seconds — this must be undeniable]
THE FIGURATIVE READ: [What the purist sees — the second layer]
THE BRIDGE: [What allows the tourist to enter without knowing, and what allows the purist to find depth without being lectured]
THE TOURIST TEST RESULT: [Who you tested, what they said]
THE PURIST TEST RESULT: [Who you tested, what they said]
THE LINE: [One sentence that captures the figure — the way you'd describe what you made to someone who'll see both layers]
```

## The Deeper Principle

A figure of speech is a phrase that means one thing literally and another thing actually. "He drowned in paperwork" — he didn't actually drown. "The deal fell through" — nothing physically fell. We use figures of speech because they carry more meaning than literal language can.

Two-level work is the same idea applied to design, product, brand, art, business. The literal layer is what you said. The figurative layer is what you meant.

The best work is dense with figures. Every choice is doing two jobs at once.

The question isn't: who is this for?

The question is: how do I make this work for the kid in Columbus AND the curator at the MCA, without compromising for either one?
